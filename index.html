The receiving towns and the migrants may benefit from accepting refugees. Through the diverse views, abilities, and experiences they offer, refugees can benefit their new communities. In return, host towns can offer refugees a secure setting to start over and chances for growth. Host towns can give immigrants access to resources for healthcare and education. In addition to improving their health and wellbeing, this can assist immigrants in learning new skills and information. Host towns can also encourage social participation by accepting migrants into their neighborhoods. This can lessen loneliness and give migrants a feeling of community. In conclusion, embracing immigrants can be advantageous to both them and the host towns. Refugees can add to their new communities by encouraging variety, talent sharing, and business. Host communities can support the success of immigrants and advance the advancement of society by giving them a secure environment in which to restore their lives and access to opportunities.

When a person has a real fear of being persecuted or is in danger in their native country because of their race, faith, or nationality in a specific social group, they should be granted refuge. The United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) defines a refugee as someone who "owing to a well-founded fear of being persecuted for reasons of race, religion, nationality, membership of a particular social group or political opinion, is outside the country of his nationality and is unable or, owing to such fear, is unwilling to avail himself of the protection of that country." Additionally, when fighting, widespread violence, or other situations that have badly interrupted public order prevent refugees from returning to their native country, they should be given the right to seek protection elsewhere. Access to essential services, legal aid, and chances for societal assimilation should be made available to refugees.

When refugees are escaping inequality, conflict, or violence in their native country and are unable to return because of their fear of further harm, they should be granted refuge.The 1951 Refugee Convention and its 1967 Protocol require that the nation where an immigrant seeks sanctuary must give that person asylum and security. The Convention also forbids states from non-refoulement, also known as returning immigrants to a nation where they may suffer persecution. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights, the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, and the Convention on the Rights of the Child are just a few of the global and regional legal frameworks that safeguard the rights of migrants. Authorities must evaluate each individual's claim and conditions to determine whether they qualify as refugees and are entitled to safety. These evaluations need to be based in international human rights and refugee law and would need to consider the individual's ethnicity, gender, sexual orientation, and other characteristics that might make them more vulnerable to abuse. In conclusion, when refugees are unable to return to their native country due to fear of additional harm, they should be given the right to seek shelter elsewhere. In accordance with international refugee and human rights laws, they should be given safety and asylum.
